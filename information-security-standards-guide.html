01
Why this matters for law firms

Law firms occupy a uniquely exposed position in the threat landscape. Client matter data — often privileged, commercially sensitive, or subject to confidentiality obligations — is your core asset and your primary target. At the same time, global operations, complex supply chains, and rapid adoption of cloud and SaaS platforms mean the attack surface is expanding faster than most security teams can keep pace with.

ISO 27001 certification provides a compliance framework, but certification alone does not protect clients or the firm. What protects the firm is a security programme that is traceable to real business risk, consumable by the teams who must implement it, and maintained as the technology landscape changes. That is what this programme is designed to deliver.

The core problem
Many law firms have security policies that exist as documents rather than as lived practice. They were written once, approved by a committee, filed in a SharePoint library, and have not been meaningfully updated since. Engineering teams either don't know they exist or cannot translate them into practical configuration decisions. This programme closes that gap.
02
Principles before practices

Before producing a single standard, your programme needs to be anchored in principles — the "why" that informs every "what." Without this foundation, standards become disconnected from business need and are perceived as academic or irrelevant by the teams who must follow them.

For a global law firm, the driving principles are:

Enable business Inspire trust Enact resilience Balance risk Create certainty & clarity Establish traceability Capitalise change & agility Simplify complexity Solve holistically Ensure sustainability

Every standard in your library should be traceable to one or more of these principles. When a standard cannot be traced to a principle, it is a signal that the standard may be providing compliance theatre rather than genuine security value. The agent, during the drafting workflow, will explicitly ask you to confirm this traceability for each document it produces.

The right question to ask
Not "are we correctly following the standard?" but "are we using the standard to achieve a secure, trustworthy outcome for the firm and its clients?" Standards are a means, not an end.
03
The two-layer model

The programme operates on two distinct but connected layers. Understanding the distinction is critical — conflating them is one of the most common reasons security documentation fails to be useful.

Information security standards
Technology-agnostic documents that define what must be done and why. Audience: architects, product owners, assurance teams, and risk managers. These are enduring documents — they should not change every time a vendor releases a new feature.
Security baselines
Technology-specific configuration guides derived from standards, defining exactly how to implement the standard in a given platform. Audience: engineering teams and platform owners. These change frequently and benefit most from agent-assisted maintenance.

Every baseline maps to one or more parent standards. Every standard maps to one or more ISO 27001:2022 Annex A controls. This chain of traceability — from ISO control, through standard, to baseline configuration item — is what makes your programme audit-ready and internally defensible.

06
Phasing your programme

Attempting to produce all 44 standards simultaneously is counterproductive. A phased approach lets you demonstrate early value to ISO auditors, build momentum with internal teams, and use learnings from early standards to improve later ones.

1
Months 1–4
Foundation — highest audit exposure & law firm-specific risk
IAM lifecycle, Data classification, Encryption, Client matter data, Incident management, Third-party risk, Patch management, Cloud security (agnostic), and Vulnerability management. Paired baselines: Entra ID, M365, Defender, and Purview. These 10 standards directly address the most likely ISO 27001 audit findings for a law firm and protect the most critical assets from day one.
2
Months 4–8
Expansion — full cloud domain, network, and regulatory coverage
Full cloud security domain (landing zone, SaaS procurement, CIEM, secrets management), remaining endpoint and network standards, the legal and regulatory compliance standard (structurally complex given multi-jurisdiction footprint), SDL if you have engineering activity, and the cross-border data transfer standard. Azure and AWS baselines produced in this phase.
3
Months 8–12
Maturity — emerging risk and operational excellence
AI and LLM usage standard (allow your firm's usage patterns to stabilise before locking down the standard), insider threat, ransomware response playbook, container and serverless security. By this phase, the agent is in maintenance mode on Phase 1 standards, producing first review reports against changed vendor and regulatory guidance.
07
The agentic workflow

The AI agent operates across three distinct workflows. Each is designed to produce a specific output with a defined human approval gate. The agent is never the final decision-maker — it is your drafter, analyst, and reviewer.

1
Provide inputs to the agent
Supply your approved standard template, the relevant ISO 27001:2022 Annex A controls, any regulatory context (e.g. SRA Principle 7, GDPR Article 32), and your organisation's context profile — law firm, global, M365/cloud-first, complex supply chain.
2
Agent produces a full draft
The agent completes every section of the template, including: purpose and scope, control requirements with rationale, a principle traceability statement, an ISO 27001 mapping table, audience guidance for each internal team, and a list of assumptions made that require human validation.
3
SME review and amendment
Your subject matter expert reviews the draft. The agent gets you 70–80% of the way there — the remaining 20–30% is where your specific environment knowledge is irreplaceable. Particular attention to: law-firm-specific clauses, jurisdiction-specific requirements, and any control decisions that reflect conscious risk acceptance.
4
Named human approval and publication
A named approver reviews and signs off. The metadata block is completed. The standard is published to the self-service repository. The approval event — with approver name, date, and version — is the ISO 27001 evidence record.
1
Start from an approved parent standard
Baseline generation is only triggered once the relevant parent standard is approved. The baseline is a derived artefact — it inherits the "why" from the standard and translates it into platform-specific "how."
2
Agent maps benchmark to standard
The agent takes the parent standard alongside the relevant vendor hardening guide (e.g. CIS Microsoft 365 Foundations Benchmark) and maps every configuration item to a requirement in the standard. Items with no parent requirement are flagged — either adopt them or create an explicit exception.
3
Every line is traceable
The output is a baseline where every configuration item carries: the configuration requirement, the rationale, the parent standard clause reference, the ISO 27001 Annex A control, and the implementation priority. This is what makes baselines audit-ready rather than just configuration checklists.
4
Engineering team validation and approval
The relevant platform engineering team validates the baseline against the actual environment. Exceptions are documented in the exception register rather than silently omitted. Approved and published with the same governance as standards.
1
Review is triggered by metadata date
Each document carries a "next review date" in its metadata block. When that date arrives, the review workflow is triggered. Cloud and M365 baselines review every six months; most standards annually.
2
Agent analyses what has changed
The agent is given the current approved document, relevant vendor changelogs and security advisories since the last review, updated ISO 27001 guidance, and the current exception register. It produces a structured review report: what has changed externally, which clauses are potentially stale, and what requires a human decision vs. what can be updated automatically.
3
Human reviews the report and decides
The document owner reviews the agent's report. Each flagged item is either accepted (agent updates the document), rejected with a rationale, or escalated as a risk item. The agent produces a revised draft incorporating approved changes.
4
Republish with updated metadata
The revised document is approved and republished. The review event is logged as an ISO 27001 evidence record. The exception register is updated to reflect any new gaps identified.
08
What the agent can and cannot do

For ISO 27001 purposes, the boundary between agent and human responsibility must be explicit and consistently observed. This is itself a control you can evidence to your auditor.

Responsibility boundary
The agent
  • Drafts standards and baselines
  • Maps controls to ISO 27001 clauses
  • Identifies gaps between baseline and benchmark
  • Analyses what has changed since last review
  • Produces exception register entries for gaps
  • Flags assumptions requiring human validation
  • Updates document metadata fields
  • Produces traceability matrices
Humans only
  • Approve and publish any document
  • Accept risk on behalf of the firm
  • Grant policy exceptions or waivers
  • Retire or deprecate a standard
  • Interpret jurisdiction-specific legal obligations
  • Make decisions with client confidentiality implications
  • Sign off ISO 27001 evidence records
  • Escalate to senior leadership or the board
Audit tip
Document this boundary in your ISMS. Your auditor will ask how AI-assisted drafting is governed. "The agent drafts; named humans approve; approval events are dated records" is a clean and defensible answer.
09
Document metadata & governance

The metadata block is the connective tissue of the entire programme. The agent reads and writes it as part of every workflow. Every standard and baseline must carry the following fields:

document_id: ISS-IAM-001
domain: Identity & Access Management
type: Standard | Baseline
owner_role: Head of Information Security Architecture
approver_role: Chief Information Security Officer
version: 1.2
status: Approved | Draft | Under Review | Deprecated
last_review_date: 2025-01-15
next_review_date: 2026-01-15
iso_27001_clauses: [ 5.15, 5.16, 5.17, 5.18 ]
related_standards: [ ISS-IAM-002, ISS-IAM-003 ]
related_baselines: [ BSL-ENTRA-001, BSL-M365-001 ]
applicable_jurisdictions: [ Global ]
exceptions_in_flight: [ EXC-2025-047 ]
principle_traceability: [ Enable Business, Inspire Trust, Create Certainty ]

Store all standards and baselines in a version-controlled repository — Confluence, SharePoint with versioning enabled, or a Git-based documentation system. The agent must be able to read the current approved version and write a proposed revision for human review. Approved versions must be immutable; only a new version number and a named approval event can supersede them.

10
ISO 27001 mapping

One of the earliest high-value outputs the agent can produce is a gap analysis showing which ISO 27001:2022 Annex A controls are covered by which standards, and which controls currently have no standard owner. This gives you a clear audit readiness view and a prioritisation tool for the programme backlog.

The agent produces this as a traceability matrix: rows are ISO 27001 controls, columns are your standards. Each cell is either covered, partially covered, or a gap. Gaps feed directly into the Phase 1 prioritisation. Partial coverage items generate a specific drafting task to add the missing clauses to an existing standard.

To get started
Share your existing example standards with the agent. It will analyse the template structure, assess current ISO 27001 coverage, and produce both a gap analysis and a draft of the next priority standard in your template format. The first draft is the hardest — once your template and context are established, subsequent standards build on that foundation rapidly.
